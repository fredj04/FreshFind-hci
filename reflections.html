Reflections & Conclusion

Lessons learned, what worked, and how FreshFind addresses user needs

What Worked Well

Evidence-First Design

Grounding every design decision in a specific source finding — rather than intuition — made the rationale clear and defensible. When the AI Agent hypotheses were contradicted by NotebookLM sources, we had the evidence to make the right call.

Dignity-First as a Design Constraint

Treating "no login, no identity exposure" as a hard constraint — not a preference — forced creative solutions (FAB for frequent contributors, anonymous quick-tap) that improved the UX for everyone, not just stigma-sensitive users.

Peer Heuristic Evaluation

The FoodPath team found 10 real issues, including two severity-3 problems (blank map on slow connections, hidden undo button) that would have significantly degraded the experience. Fresh eyes matter.

Storyboards as Design Anchors

Having specific user scenarios (Joe, Marie, Steven) kept design decisions grounded in real use cases. When evaluating changes, asking "would this work for Joe?" was more useful than abstract principles.

What Could Be Improved

Real User Testing

All evaluation was peer-based, not with actual food desert residents. The most important next step is testing with real users in the South Bronx, East Harlem, and Central Brooklyn neighborhoods identified in Phase 2.

Transit Integration Depth

The route-based "Stop on My Way" filter was defined conceptually but not fully prototyped. Integrating real transit API data (MTA for NYC) would require significant technical validation beyond the scope of this phase.

Next Steps

If FreshFind were to continue into a Phase 4, the priorities would be: (1) usability testing with 5 real users from target neighborhoods; (2) technical feasibility assessment for MTA transit API integration; (3) partnership outreach to NYC food pantries for listing data; (4) accessibility audit (screen reader compatibility, color contrast verification across full prototype).
