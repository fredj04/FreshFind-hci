Reflections & Conclusion

Lessons learned, what worked, and how FreshFind addresses user needs

What Worked Well

Evidence-First Design Decisions

Grounding every design decision in a specific research finding — rather than intuition — made every choice defensible. When the AI Agent hypotheses were contradicted by NotebookLM sources (e.g., the agent thought real-time accuracy was "nice to have"), the evidence made it easy to reject that assumption and prioritize correctly.

Dignity-First as a Hard Constraint

Treating "no login, no identity exposure" as a non-negotiable constraint — not just a preference — produced better design outcomes. The 5-minute undo timer, the anonymous quick-tap menu, and the "Posted anonymously — no account needed" reassurance all came from taking this constraint seriously.

Peer Heuristic Evaluation

The heuristic evaluation by Glenn Miller and others identified issues I had not noticed, especially H2 (language names in own script) and H3 (undo after status update). Both were severity 3 — high enough to require attention — and both were addressed before the final prototype. Fresh eyes consistently find what you stop seeing.

Concrete User Scenarios

Having specific user scenarios — Joe needing food on his way home, Marie trying to read the app in Spanish, Steven wanting to warn neighbors — kept every design decision grounded. When evaluating a change, asking "would this work for Steven?" was more useful than asking "is this a good UX pattern in general?"

What Could Be Improved

Real User Testing

All evaluation was done by peers, friends, and family — not actual food desert residents. The most important next step is testing with 5 real users from the South Bronx, East Harlem, and Central Brooklyn neighborhoods identified in Phase 2 research. Their feedback would likely surface friction points that no amount of peer review can predict.

Transit Integration

The "En mi ruta / On My Way" filter was defined conceptually and appears in the prototype, but integrating real MTA transit schedule data would require significant technical work beyond the scope of this course. A future version would need to validate feasibility with the MTA's GTFS data feed and test with users who rely on public transit.

How the Final Prototype Addresses Research Findings

Research FindingDesign Response in PrototypeStatus
F1: Stale data causes wasted trips Every listing shows a community-verified timestamp ("Verified 9 min ago by a community member"). Submit button is disabled until a status is selected, preventing empty submissions. ✅ Resolved
F2: Route-based discovery needed "En mi ruta / On My Way" filter pill visible in the map and list views. Conceptually designed; ready for backend implementation. ✅ Designed
F3: Food Mirage / affordability SNAP/EBT accepted badge visible on every listing card and filterable from the map home screen. Users can immediately filter for affordable resources. ✅ Resolved
F4: Transit deserts overlap food deserts Directions view shows walking routes with estimated time and open status visible at the destination. "On My Way" filter addresses commute-route discovery. ✅ Designed
F5: Stigma suppresses use Zero login anywhere in the app. "Posted anonymously — no account needed" confirmation shown on every status submission. No contributor identity ever displayed. ✅ Resolved
F6: Community trust beats formal listings Anonymous one-tap updates with prominent timestamp. 5-minute undo prevents bad data. Submit disabled until selection made, preventing accidental empty posts. ✅ Resolved

Next Steps

If FreshFind were to continue into a Phase 4, the priorities would be: (1) usability testing with 5 real users from food desert neighborhoods — South Bronx, East Harlem, Central Brooklyn; (2) MTA GTFS data integration for the "On My Way" route filter; (3) partnership outreach to NYC food pantries for listing data seeding; (4) accessibility audit for screen reader compatibility and WCAG color contrast compliance; (5) offline mode implementation for low-connectivity environments.
