Project Overview

Context, problem, target users, and key features

The Problem: Food Deserts

Approximately 19 million Americans live in food deserts — areas where residents have limited access to affordable, nutritious food. These areas are disproportionately low-income and urban. In New York City alone, 1.3 million residents face this reality. When grocery stores leave for more profitable areas, residents are left relying on fast food, dollar stores, or spending significant time and transit fare traveling for basic necessities.

Existing tools fail these communities in predictable ways: USDA databases are rarely updated, Google Maps lacks dedicated food pantry categories, and no existing app provides real-time, community-verified status in multiple languages. There are also harder-to-see problems — the "Food Mirage" (nearby stores with unaffordable prices) and the "Food Swamp" (neighborhoods saturated with cheap processed options) — that a map alone cannot solve.

Target Users

Primary Users

Food Desert Residents

Low-income adults (25–65) in USDA-designated food deserts, especially in NYC neighborhoods like the South Bronx, East Harlem, and Central Brooklyn. Includes non-English speakers (particularly Spanish-speaking households) and elderly users with limited smartphone experience.

Secondary Users

Community Contributors

Residents who pass by food resources regularly and can submit quick anonymous status updates — "Out of produce," "Closed today," "Long line." No account required. Their contributions are what make the platform trustworthy.

Stakeholders

Pantry Staff & Operators

Food pantry staff, mobile van operators, and social workers who refer clients to food resources. They benefit from accurate, real-time data as much as users do — and can claim listings to post official updates.

Key Features

Real-Time Community Map

Color-coded AND shape-differentiated pins: green circle with checkmark (open), orange square with exclamation (low stock), red diamond with X (closed). A floating legend is always visible. Every listing shows a community-verified timestamp so users know exactly how fresh the data is.

Anonymous Status Updates

One-tap status reporting — no account, no login, under 10 seconds. Four options: Open, Closing Soon, Closed, Out of Fresh Produce. A 5-minute undo timer ("Undo available for 4:56") protects against accidental or incorrect submissions.

Multilingual Support

Language selection on first launch in 8 languages, each shown in its own script: English, Español, Français, Kreyòl ayisyen (Haitian Creole), 中文, Português, Soomaali, Tiếng Việt. The entire app — filters, listing names, community notes, navigation instructions — renders in the selected language.

Route-Based Discovery

"En mi ruta" / "On My Way" filter finds food resources along a user's existing commute route — not just by proximity. Based on research finding that residents shop near work and transit routes, not near home.

SNAP/EBT Tags

All listings display SNAP/EBT acceptance status as a filterable badge. Addresses the "Food Mirage" finding: a store being nearby doesn't mean it's affordable. Users can filter for SNAP-accepting locations from the map home screen.

List View

In addition to the map, a List tab shows all nearby resources in order of distance with status badges, community notes, and SNAP tags visible at a glance — for users who prefer scanning text over reading a map.
