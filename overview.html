Project Overview

Context, problem, users, and design goals

The Problem: Food Deserts

Approximately 19 million Americans live in food deserts — areas where residents have limited access to affordable, nutritious food. These areas are disproportionately low-income and urban. In New York City alone, 1.3 million residents face this reality. When grocery stores leave for more profitable areas, residents are left with fast food and dollar stores — or spend significant time and transit fare traveling for basic necessities.

Existing tools fail these communities in predictable ways: USDA databases are rarely updated, Google Maps lacks food pantry categories, and no app provides real-time, community-verified status in multiple languages.

Target Users

Primary Users

Food Desert Residents

Low-income adults (25–65) in USDA-designated food deserts. Includes non-English speakers (especially Spanish-speaking households) and elderly users with limited smartphone experience.

Secondary Users

Community Contributors

Residents who pass by food resources regularly and can submit quick status updates. No account required — contribution is anonymous by design.

Stakeholders

Pantry Staff & Operators

Food pantry staff, mobile van operators, and social workers who manage listings and refer clients. They benefit from accurate, real-time data as much as users do.

Key Features

Real-Time Community Map

Color-coded pins: green (open), yellow (closing soon), orange (out of stock), gray (closed). Every listing shows a community-verified timestamp so users know how fresh the data is.

Anonymous Status Updates

One-tap status reporting — no account, no login, under 10 seconds. Updates are live immediately. A 5-minute undo window protects against accidental submissions.

Multilingual Support

Language selection on first launch in 7 languages: English, Español, Kreyòl, 中文, العربية, Français, Русский. The entire app renders in the selected language — not just labels.

Route-Based Discovery

"Stop on My Way" filter finds food resources along a user's existing commute route — not just proximity. Based on research showing routine persistence among food desert residents.

SNAP/EBT Tags

All listings include SNAP/EBT acceptance status as a filterable tag. Addresses the "Food Mirage" finding: a store being nearby doesn't mean its prices are accessible.

Low-Bandwidth Optimization

Skeleton screen animations on load, minimal asset weights, and offline-friendly caching. Designed to work on older Android phones with limited data plans.
